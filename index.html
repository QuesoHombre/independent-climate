Greenland Ice Watch
For my art review I decided to tackle climate change as a representational issue. The problem with climate change representation is it is very difficult to portray scale. Climate change affects the world inversely, meaning those who create the most greenhouse emissions are generally affected the least which makes climate change a very unnuanced issue for those to blame. We see this in the changing environment of the Inuit people, who contribute almost nothing to the changing climate, yet face some of the greatest threats of disposition of anyone. Similarly, the Greenlandic landscape faces drastic climatological challenges, which in themselves are capable of providing devastating change.

In the next 100 - 200 years, it is expected that the majority of glaciers in Greenland may recede completely. Considering Greenland is about 2.16 million km2 in size (80% of that being glacier), the continent holds a very significant amount of the world's oceans in said in glaciers. The melting of these glaciers doesn't just mean the rising of sea levels, which by themselves would completely submerge almost all of Florida and several territories completely, but also would cause the earth to spin slower, causing days to be slightly longer on average.

The Polar Science Center, an origination that conducts basic and applied research on the oceanography, climatology, meteorology, biology, and ecology of the ice-covered regions on Earth, has been tracking the melting of Greenland glacial regions since 1980. This site is an interactive medium for viewing and interpreting said data and a helpful way to put into perspective what is happening to the climate of this region.
